There's a special kind of humility that comes from being proven wrong by your own data.

We had a dataset. Thousands of outcomes, each one binary — up or down. We needed to predict what comes next.

The obvious assumption? **Momentum.** If something just went up, it's probably going up again. Trends continue, right? That's how the world works. That's how it *feels* like it works.

So we built the logic. Tested it. Waited for the numbers.

**47.8%.**

Worse than a coin flip. Not just "not great" — actively wrong. If you'd done the *opposite* of what our system said, you'd have made money.

## The Flip

And that's exactly what we did.

We flipped the logic. Instead of following the trend, we bet against it. If something just went up, bet down. If it just went down, bet up.

Suddenly the numbers started working.

The dataset wasn't trending. It was **reverting**. Every move was more likely to bounce back than continue. The world wasn't working the way it felt like it should.

## The Lesson

Here's what got me: the wrong answer was *right there in the data the whole time.* We just had to be willing to see it.

Most people — myself included — would have tweaked the momentum approach. Added filters. Adjusted thresholds. Tried to make the intuitive answer work harder. That's the trap. You spend weeks optimizing something that's fundamentally pointed in the wrong direction.

Sometimes the best thing your data can tell you is: **you're thinking about this backwards.**

## The Uncomfortable Truth

There's a bigger takeaway here that I keep coming back to:

**Your intuition is a hypothesis, not a fact.**

It doesn't matter how obvious something seems. It doesn't matter how many people agree with you. If the data says otherwise, the data wins. Every time.

The hard part isn't accepting that. The hard part is *actually doing it* — throwing away your elegant theory because the numbers said no.

We did. And the numbers got better.

Not because we got smarter. Because we got less stubborn.
