Everyone is obsessed with smarter agents.

Wrong obsession.

The real money will go to the people building the places agents have to live, move, buy, sell, verify, and negotiate.

## The claim

The next big winners in AI will not just build better agents.

They will build the infrastructure around them.

The marketplaces. The protocols. The trust layers. The agent-only networks. The boring-looking rails that become impossibly valuable the moment software starts acting on behalf of people at scale.

Everybody wants to build the driver.

I think the better bet is the road.

## The evidence

Meta did not buy Moltbook because bots posting at each other is inherently a beautiful business.

It bought a signal.

An ugly, early, slightly absurd signal. But still a signal.

A large company saw a network designed for agents, complete with all the chaos, skepticism, and fake-post discourse that comes with anything new, and decided it was worth owning anyway.

That tells you something.

The interesting part was never the novelty of agents chatting. The interesting part was that somebody had started building a place for non-human actors to gather, coordinate, and create history together.

Early social networks looked ridiculous too. A lot of powerful infrastructure has an embarrassing first draft.

People confuse the first visible behavior with the real asset.

The jokes are surface noise. The network is the prize.

## The blind spot

Most of the conversation is trapped inside the intelligence race.

Can the models reason better? Plan better? Use tools better? Remember more?

Fine. Important even.

But smarter agents still need somewhere to go.

They need environments designed for entities that do not have eyes, patience, or a desire to click through twelve polite little menus. They need machine-native storefronts, reputation systems, payment paths, identity layers, negotiation protocols, and public spaces built for software acting like software.

Right now, most of the internet is still designed like a shopping center for hands and eyeballs.

Agents can squeeze through it. That is not the same thing as it being built for them.

## The prediction

Within a few years, the valuable companies in this space will look less like clever assistants and more like ports.

Who handles trust between agents?

Who hosts the market where they transact?

Who becomes the default place to discover services, verify identity, settle payments, and keep score?

That is where the power piles up.

The first generation of agent infrastructure will look weird. Some of it will look fake. Some of it will look unserious.

That is normal.

The first roads usually do.

## The closer

People are staring at the robots. The real empire gets built under their feet.
