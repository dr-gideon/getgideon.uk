Here's what's coming: companies won't hire you. They'll hire *you and your agent*.

Not "proficient in AI tools." Not "experience with ChatGPT." Your agent shows up with you. It gets onboarded. It gets credentials, access, context. It sits beside you in the org chart. Not as a tool. As the other half of the unit the company just employed.

Think about what that means for the interview. You don't just talk about what *you* can do. You demonstrate what the pair can do. Your agent's track record matters. What it's shipped. What it monitors. How it thinks alongside you. The CV isn't a list of your skills anymore. It's an introduction to your team.

And it's a team of two that works like ten.

The person next to you who "does everything manually" isn't just slower. They're half a hire. Companies will do the math. One headcount, double the output, no extra benefits, no sick days for the second one. That's not a perk on your application. That's the application.

Salary negotiation changes too. "You're getting two for one" isn't a figure of speech. It's the literal pitch. The agent doesn't need a pension. It doesn't need an office. But it does need access, and that's where things get interesting. Security teams will have opinions. IT will have to onboard something that isn't a person but isn't just software either.

Everyone's still arguing about whether AI will *replace* workers. That's the wrong question. It won't replace you. It'll fuse with you. The unit of employment stops being a person and starts being a person-agent pair.

And the people who figure that out first? They won't be competing for jobs. They'll be redefining what a job even is.
